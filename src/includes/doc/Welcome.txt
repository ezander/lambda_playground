#*
  Welcome to the λ playground!

  This is a full-fledged environment for experimenting with the untyped λ
  calculus. It grew out of the wish to really experiment with those pesky
  definitions for booleans, numerals, pairs, lists, combinators, recursion and
  so on. Of course, you can do some stuff with paper and pencil, but that's not
  really fun, and I guess nobody has ever gone through all the steps of e.g.
  evaluating `factorial 4`. It's also deeply satisfying to be able to write the
  definitions down and see how they reduce or whether two weird and completely
  different looking terms result in the equivalent normal forms.

  When you want to try those things automatically, you are then mostly referred
  to functional languages like Haskell or theorem provers like Coq or Agda or
  the like. But those are difficult beasts, and they usually have a very
  different syntax that you have to learn first, and they usually have a type
  system, that, well, keeps you from doing bogus things, but also from all the
  flexibility and fun that the lack of a type system brings. Thus, I wanted to
  have an environment where I can enter any untyped λ formula and evaluate it
  and do whatever I like, close to the original λ syntax, but made exact by some
  grammar (you'll find the EBNF in the Help). And, here we are, a joint effort
  of myself and Claude Code in implementing such a system. I hope you have fun!

  I've created a couple of docs and tutorials on how that all works and the
  basis of λ calculus. You can just click the links below or select them from
  the pickers in the toolbar.

  In the following you'll see a simple example of the so-called Church-encoded
  booleans (if you don't know what that is yet, you'll find out in the
  tutorials): It just defines `true`, `false` and `not` in λ calculus and in the
  last line, evaluates the expression `not true`. When you go to the eval panel
  (or just hit F5) you can see how this expression is evaluated/reduced either
  in one go or step by step. And in the end gives you - as expected - the value
  `false`.
*#

true  := λx y. x
false := λx y. y
not p := p false true
not true
