#* 
  Normalization
  =============

  The λ playground often tries to bring λ expressions into normal form. Normal
  form means that no β-substitutions (i.e. "function calls" more or less) can be
  performed any more on the expression. Here is an example of some expression
  applied to the identity

     (λ x . x)(λ y . y a)
     x[x:=(λ y . y a)]     # replace x in the leftmost abstraction with the 
                           # argument, i.e., (λ y . y a)
     (λ y . y a)

  given the `(λ y . y a)` as result, where nothing can be substituted any more.
  This would be the normal form of the original expression `(λ x . x)(λ y . y)`.
  Note that naming of bound variables does not matter: `(λ z . z a)` would 
  count as normal form just as well (this is called α-equivalence). 

  There are some things to note here:
    a) Not all expressions have a normal form. E.g. `(λ x . x x)(λ x . x x)` 
       (which is sometimes called the Ω combinator) does not have a normal form: 
       every substitution just leads back to the original expression.
    b) Some expressions even grow indefinetely like `(λ x . x x)(λ x . x x x)`
       Just copy it somewhere and try to expand it β-step by β-step.
    c) Some expressions have relatively small normal forms, but huge 
       intermeditate representation, e.g. try to evaluate 
       `/ (factorial 10) (factorial 9)` with Church numerals (or better: don't)
    d) There is no method to determine whether a general λ expression has a 
       normal form at all (this was at the center of the Entscheidungsproblem
       and the halting problem, solved back then in the 1930s by Church and 
       Turing)
    e) Even if there is a normal form, it cannot be predetermined in how many 
       steps this can be achieved and how big the intermediate expressions become.

  Given these complications, in a computational system like this here, we must
  decide or terminate at some step. Therefore, there is a step limit for 
  substitutions and also a size limit for intermediate expression, that can be 
  controlled either via the settings or in code via pragmas [doc/Pragmas]. The
  step limit is usually set to 1000, i.e. after 1000 some "did not terminate"
  message will pop up in the output, or when an intermediate has more than 
  a set limit (default: 3000) terms in it's abstract syntax tree (AST), the 
  evaluation will also be stopped, in order to preserve memory. You can change
  that globally via the settings, or via pragmas like `max-steps` or `max-size`.

  Lambda expressions appearing alone on a line are not normalized by default.
  Definitions on the contrary are, because that usually speeds up things for
  future use. For definitions you know, won't normalize (e.g. the Ω combinator
  `(λx.x x)(λx.x x)`), or you just don't want normalization to happen you can
  disable it via `no-normalize-defs` and re-enable via `normalize-defs`. T


  Leftmost-outermost:

  weak normalization 
  using defs for speed optimization

  Here are some examples concerning normalization and equivalences:
*#


# I I normalizes to I
I := λx . x 
≡ (I I) I

# Ω has no normal form
ω := λx . x x
≢ (ω ω) (ω ω)
#! no-normalize-defs
Ω := ω ω
#! normalize-defs

# λ x . z x is only η-equivalent to z
≢ (λ x . z x) z
#! allow-eta
≡ (λ x . z x) z
#! no-allow-eta

# K I Ω has a weak normal form, that can be found via leftmost-outermost 
# reductions, though Ω itself has no normal form
K y := λ x . y
≡ I (K I Ω)
