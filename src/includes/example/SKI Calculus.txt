# We define the following combinators S and K:

S := λf g x. f x (g x)
K := λx y. x

# which we could also have imported from ("std/Combinators")
# The interesting thing about them, is that essentially everything
# you can do with ULC, you can do with just those two combinators.
# Without going into the theory, we just show some things, e.g.
# that we can express the identity only by S and K

# Define I by only S and K
I := S K K
# and indeed it's the identity
≡ I (λx. x)


# Then we can "prove" also the following identities
# (see e.g. [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SKI_combinator_calculus])

≡ ((S K I)(K I S)) I
≡ (K S(I(S K S I))) S
≡ (S K I K) K

# Or we can define booleans in terms of just S and K
# Define true (T) and false (F)
T := K
F := S K

# The definition of `not` is similar to the Church booleans (which is
# of course, because T and F reduce to the exact same normal form...)
not b := b F T
≡ (not T) F
≡ (not F) T

# And then `and` or `or` are of cause also the same as for Church booleans
or a b := a T b
π[a:={T,F},b:={T,F}] or a b

and a b := a b F
π[a:={T,F},b:={T,F}] and a b
