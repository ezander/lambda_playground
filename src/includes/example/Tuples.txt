#*
  Church pairs are usually defined as follows

    pair a b f := f a b
    first p := p (λa b. a)
    second p := p (λa b. b)

  We just import the definitions from here...
*#

:import "std/Pairs"

#*
  How could we define triples? Just the same, now only with three args...
*#

triple a b c f := f a b c

#*
  One problem we get here, is the naming. The problem is that if we define

    first t := t (λa b c.a)

  we can't use it for pairs any more. So, we need to use a different name. E.g.
  we could choose first/3 to indicate, we choose the first of 3. But of course
  you could choose any other naming convention
*#

first/3 t := t (λa b c. a)
second/3 t := t (λa b c. b)
third/3 t := t (λa b c. c)

# An example using them (note: we haven't imported any numerals, so the numbers
# you see, are just free variable with the names `12`, `3` and `6` without
# further meaning.)
3d_coords := triple 12 3 6
≡ (first/3 3d_coords) 12
≡ (second/3 3d_coords) 3
≡ (third/3 3d_coords) 6

# If you use pairs and triples often that may feel clumsy. Now, you can just
# import lists for that, or we can brew our own here, and define any kind of
# tuple with it.

singleton a := λ f . f a dummy
pair a b ::= λ f . f a (singleton b)
triple a b c ::= λ f . f a (pair b c)

rest t := t (λ a b. b)
first t ::= t (λa b. a)
second t ::= first (rest t)
third t := first (rest (rest t))

# Now our definitions work for 3d
3d_coords ::= triple 12 3 6
≡ (first 3d_coords) 12
≡ (second 3d_coords) 3
≡ (third 3d_coords) 6

# and for 2d
2d_coords := pair 4 5
≡ (first 2d_coords) 4
≡ (second 2d_coords) 5
